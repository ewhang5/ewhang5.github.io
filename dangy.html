Dangy
Problem

According to the NY Post, we waste 10,368 hours of our lives deciding what to eat. To solve this problem, 185M people spend hours searching through Google or Yelp, only to order a dish that often doesn’t fit their tastes. This is because a high restaurant rating doesn’t mean you’ll order something you like as these platforms don’t know your taste profile. To exacerbate this problem, many people are not introduced to new restaurants or dishes by the restaurants themselves, because most restaurants “only promote to existing customers”. This is because advertising is unaffordable for most restaurants, especially small, independent restaurants. Yelp for Business and Google AdWords use a bidding process that can drive the cost-per-click to $40! This is unaffordable, especially when 85% of businesses rated Yelp for Business one-star and Forbes says that “AdWords just doesn’t fit.”

The Solution

Introducing Dangy, a mobile app that custom-recommends restaurant dishes you’ll love! Dangy uses AI to assess your taste profile, crowdsource restaurant dish reviews, find users with similar tastes as you, and analyze ingredients in dishes to recommend the best ones for you! Unlike our competitors, Dangy recommends dishes fit for your tastes so you can confidently order without spending hours searching. After, Dangy curates a list of the top dishes you need to try, Dang-it! Rate dishes on taste, spiciness, and value, and share fun, TikTok-like videos! For restaurants, Dangy offers affordable advertising at 5% of the dish price. Dangy’s dish ads are also the most effective because we match them with users who would love those dishes to increase click-through and conversion rates. Finally, we offer restaurants a subscription service to answer their biggest question: “Why don’t customers like certain dishes?” We increase customer satisfaction and retention.

Technical Aspect

To build Dangy, I used React Native and JavaScript for the frontend and incorporated 12 AWS services for a scalable backend, including S3, Lambda and DynamoDB for media storage, ad hoc serverless compute, and millisecond response-time database querying. For the AI model, I employed vector quantization with K-Means Clustering and logistic regression to draw relationships between users’ taste profiles; Word2Vec, which uses Natural Language Processing to map word associations for dish descriptions; and, an Actor-Critic Reinforcement Learning model to improve recommendation accuracy.
